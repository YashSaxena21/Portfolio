About Me

I'm Yash Saxena, a Ph.D. student in Computer Science at the University of Maryland, Baltimore County. My research sits at the intersection of retrieval-augmented generation, interpretability, and source attribution. I focus on making each stage of a language agent (retrieval, evidence selection, and generation) transparent and verifiable, through interpretable retrievers, rationale-driven evidence selection, and citation schemes that balance coverage with correctness. I care about language models that can show their work and earn trust in domains where mistakes actually matter.

Before my Ph.D., I completed a B.Tech. in Computer Science and Engineering (AI & ML) at Galgotias University. I have worked on projects in mental health classification, game content generation, and legal tech, in roles that combine research and engineering. I enjoy mentoring undergraduate researchers, collaborating with interdisciplinary teams, and turning ideas into practical tools. Outside of research, you will usually find me exploring music, reading about neuroscience, or at a hackathon.

Generation-Time vs. Post-hoc Citation

In this work, we investigate two paradigms for citing sources in generated text: Generation-Time Citation (G-Cite) and Post-hoc Citation (P-Cite). G-Cite annotates evidence as text is produced, whereas P-Cite retrospectively links generated answers to retrieved documents. Through experiments on multiple attribution datasets we show that citation quality is largely governed by the quality of retrieval. P-Cite yields higher coverage and competitive correctness compared to G-Cite, demonstrating that post-hoc approaches can provide more complete attribution while remaining precise. Our analysis suggests adopting a retrieval-centric, P-Cite-first strategy in high-stakes domains, where transparency and completeness of citations are paramount.

Evaluating Consistency & Reasoning of LLMs

This paper benchmarks the consistency and reasoning abilities of large language models using the Boolq dataset. We assess both public and proprietary models across metrics including BERT-score, BLEU and F-1 on generated explanations and answers. Results reveal that proprietary models outperform open-source alternatives; however, no model excels at both consistency and reasoning simultaneously. The findings underscore gaps in current evaluation and highlight the need for models that can both reason and maintain internal consistency across tasks.

Can LLMs Obfuscate Code?

Addressing a growing cybersecurity question, this work introduces MetamorphASM, a benchmark of 328,200 obfuscated assembly code snippets, to evaluate whether LLMs can generate obfuscated malware. We test models under various obfuscation techniques, including dead code insertion, register substitution and control-flow manipulation, and find that LLMs are capable of producing obfuscated code that challenges static analysis tools. The study emphasises the dual-use nature of LLMs and calls for developing safeguards to mitigate malicious applications.

Emotion-Based Mental Health Classifier

This paper presents a sentiment-driven machine learning pipeline for identifying the mental state of engineering students from social media posts. By combining sentiment analysis with recurrent neural networks (RNNs), gated recurrent units (GRUs) and support vector machines (SVMs), the system classifies emotions into categories such as anxiety, joy and sadness. The approach seeks to facilitate early detection of mental health concerns and encourage proactive support within academic communities.
